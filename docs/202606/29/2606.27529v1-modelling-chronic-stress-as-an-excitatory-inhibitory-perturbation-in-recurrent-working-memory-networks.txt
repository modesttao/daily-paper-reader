ABSTRACT 

Stress is an adaptive response coordinated by neural and physiological systems. While acute stress can enhance survival, chronic stress drives structural brain changes, cognitive dysfunction, and increased psychiatric risk. At the cellular level, chronic stress shifts the excitatory-inhibitory (E/I) balance of prefrontal pyramidal neurons toward inhibitory dominance, yet the mechanisms underlying these alterations are still unknown. We here investigate possible mechanisms causing inhibitory dominance using recurrent neuronal networks trained on a working memory task. Chronic stress is modelled as a modulation in synaptic strength or neuronal activity, systematically comparing eight candidate operators against three experimentally motivated signatures of stress-induced prefrontal dysfunction: inhibitory dominance, excitatory hypofunction, and impaired task performance. These signatures are all recovered by a single stress mechanism, stronger inhibitory-to-excitatory synapses. Contrasting naive networks with resilient networks trained under the stress mechanism, we find that resilience training not only preserves task performance under stress, but also confines the network to the same dynamical subspace and energetic regime with and without stress. This resilience comes at a cost: resilient networks generalise less well when the task requires longer memory than seen during training, indicating that resilient networks find a specialised solution tuned to the trained regime. This trade-off between resilience and generalization performance persists across stress magnitude and network size, offering a computational analogue of the shift toward rigid, habit-like behaviour reported in animal following chronic stress.

1 Introduction 

Chronic stress affects individuals across multiple scales of neural organisation, impairing circuit dynamics, structural integrity, cognitive and affective function, as well as behaviour [ 1, 2]. The mechanisms underlying these detrimental effects, and the causal relations between them, are not yet completely understood. Computational models of neural circuits that incorporate biologically grounded constraints offer a principled framework to systematically isolate and compare candidate stress mechanisms. The literature shows that chronic stress impairs cognitive and affective functions that depend on the prefrontal cortex (PFC) and hippocampus. Prolonged stress exposure weakens working memory, cognitive flexibility, declarative memory, and spatial navigation, while promoting rigid, habit-driven behavioural strategies [ 3, 4 , 5, 6, 7 ]. At the cellular and circuit levels, these functional changes are accompanied by structural remodelling of prefrontal pyramidal neurons, including dendritic retraction, debranching, and spine loss [ 8], as well as by dysregulated corticolimbic and hypothalamic circuits involved in threat processing, emotional learning, and stress-hormone control [1, 9, 10]. A central candidate mechanism for this stress-induced reorganisation is disruption of excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) balance within prefrontal circuits. E/I balance refers to the functional equilibrium between excitatory glutamatergic and inhibitory GABAergic signalling, which is essential for stable yet flexible information processing [ 11 ]. In the PFC, this balance supports working memory, decision-making, planning, error monitoring, and emotional regulation, and its disruption is implicated in several neuropsychiatric conditions, including major depressive disorder and anxiety disorders [ 12 , 13 , 14 ]. In the context of chronic stress, converging evidence indicates that prolonged exposure shifts medial PFC circuitry toward reduced excitatory output or inhibitory dominance, thereby weakening prefrontal control over cognition and behaviour. Experimental studies support the E/I balance as possible stress mechanism but also reveal that stress-induced E/I disruption does not follow a single uniform mechanism. In adult male rats, chronic stress increased inhibition in the infralimbic mPFC by enhancing presynaptic GABA release and increasing GABAergic contacts onto pyramidal neurons, an effect associated with selective loss of glucocorticoid receptors in parvalbumin (PV) interneurons [ 15 ]. In adult mice, chronic unpredictable stress increased the inhibitory/excitatory ratio in layer 5/6 pyramidal neurons across the mPFC through subregion-specific synaptic changes: inhibitory drive increased in the prelimbic cortex, whereas excitatory drive decreased in the infralimbic cortex [ 16 ]. Whether prefrontal hypofunction reflects elevated inhibitory drive, weakened excitation, disinhibition of specific output pathways, or some combination of these mechanisms, and how such shifts propagate through local PV-mediated inhibition and long-range PFC projections, remain open questions [15 , 17 , 18 , 1, 19 , 18 ]. Stress-induced prefrontal dysfunction likely reflects a family of circuit perturbations rather than a single causal pathway, motivating modelling frameworks that can systematically disentangle these mechanisms. We here use recurrent neuronal networks (RNNs) trained on a working memory task to elucidate possible mechanisms underlying these observations. Computational models offer fully observable and controllable systems in which candidate mechanisms can be isolated, combined, and selectively ablated. Previous work using spiking circuit models, mean-field analyses, and recurrent networks has shown that E/I balance shapes evidence accumulation, attractor stability, and competition between neural representations, and that inhibition contributes actively to persistent activity and competitive dynamics rather than acting purely as a suppressive force [ 20 , 21 ]. Task-trained recurrent networks have also become a useful framework for studying working-memory computation, including the maintenance and manipulation of information through persistent activity, short-term synaptic mechanisms, dynamic coding, structured population manifolds, and biologically grounded constraints such as Dale’s principle [ 22 , 23 , 24 , 25 , 26 ]. Recent work has begun connecting stress and neuromodulation to recurrent computation [ 27 , 28 ], and related studies of noise injection and weight perturbation have examined regularisation and robustness in RNNs [ 29 , 30 , 31 ]. However, existing work has not systematically tested which biologically motivated E/I perturbations best reproduce experimental signatures of stress-induced prefrontal dysfunction. Here, we constrain all-to-all connected RNNs by Dale’s law, such that all synaptic connections arising from one neuron are either excitatory or inhibitory. Previous studies have shown that this constraint shapes network dynamics: analyses of the spectral properties of the recurrent weight matrix and of network function indicate that excitatory-inhibitory networks suppress oscillatory dynamics and respond faster to stimuli than their unconstrained counterparts [ 32 , 33 ]. We train the networks on a working-memory task, in which the network has to compare two stimuli shown with a variable delay between both, S1 and S2, and report the higher stimulus [ 22 , 34 , 35 ], see Fig. 1. Working-memory maintenance offers a natural test bed, because it depends on recurrent dynamics shaped by the balance between excitation and inhibition [36, 37]. We model chronic stress in these networks as a circuit-level perturbation, with the aim to reproduce the shift in the E/I ratio toward inhibitory dominance, experimentally observed in prefrontal pyramidal neurons during stress-induced 2Chronic stress as an E/I perturbation in RNNs A P REPRINT 

prefrontal dysfunction, as introduced above. We formalise stress as an operator S that selectively enhances synaptic connectivity or neuronal activity, and systematically compare different candidate mechanisms. Networks trained solely on the working memory task, called naive networks in the following, are highly sensitive to the stress operators. To enhance robustness, we introduced a resilience training motivated by the observation that animals are most resilient against stress when subjected to a moderate amount of stress during development (stress inoculation, [38 ]): we trained networks on the memory task while simultaneously employing the stress operator. These networks maintained task performance under stress, and we call them, following the stress-resilience literature [ 39 ], resilient networks . Note that, from a network-theoretical point of view, our resilient networks might be seen, due to their lack of plasticity after training, rather as robust than resilient to the stress operator. We find that selectively enhancing inhibitory synaptic strength onto excitatory neurons is the best fitting stress candidate mechanism, and that resilience comes at a cost: Resilient networks generalise less well to delays longer than those used during training (out-of-distribution delays), while naive networks retain stronger delay generalisation even under stress. 2 Results 

Excitatory-inhibitory recurrent network model and working memory task. Following the work by Song et al. [22] and others [ 40 , 23 , 34 , 35 ], our networks consist of 200 rate-based neurons, 80% excitatory and 20% inhibitory, unless stated otherwise; further details are given in Section 4.1 and a representative network schematic is shown in Fig. 1(a). Neurons are all-to-all connected according to the connectivity matrix W , consisting of submatrices WE→E ,

WE→I , WI→E , WI→I . The activity of the neurons is denoted by rE and rI for excitatory and inhibitory populations, respectively. The output of the network is decoded solely by the excitatory population, motivated by the predominance of excitatory connections between brain areas. To evaluate the excitatory-inhibitory balance, we introduce the excitatory drive hE as sum over recurrent input of the excitatory population, and the inhibitory drive hI accordingly, both defined in Eq. 2 in the Methods 4.1. We trained the networks on a delayed parametric comparison task, in which two stimuli S1 and S2 presented with temporal delay need to be compared, and the larger stimulus is reported by the network output: Besides recurrent input, neurons receive three independent, scalar inputs, two representing the stimuli S1 and S2 and the remaining one communicating the timing of the network output. The two scalar stimuli, S1 and S2, are presented separated by a variable delay, and the network must report whether S1 > S 2 or S2 > S 1 through a continuous regression target. Trial difficulty is controlled by the stimulus difference ∆ = S2 − S1, with small |∆| producing the most ambiguous trials. The full structure of a trial is illustrated in Fig. 1(b), implementation details can be found in Section 4.2. The task isolates three core cognitive functions: encoding sensory information, maintaining it across the delay between the presentation of S1 and S2, and making a decision from the integrated evidence. By sampling task difficulty ∆ across a range of values we can measure how performance degrades as the comparison becomes more ambiguous, yielding a psychometric curve, and how the internal dynamics adapt to preserve decision accuracy across difficulty. The delay period lets us probe parametric working memory directly, by examining how the network holds the first stimulus S1 in its recurrent activity and how well it generalises to out-of-distribution delays. 

Stress operators and target signatures. As outlined in the introduction, chronic stress disrupts E/I balance in prefrontal circuits, with converging evidence from rodent studies indicating a shift toward inhibitory dominance and weakened excitatory output of pyramidal neurons [ 15 , 16 , 14 ]. Inhibitory dominance can result from different mechanisms, increased inhibitory synaptic strength or neuronal activity, or decreased excitatory synaptic strength or neuronal activity. We implement these alternatives as stress operators S into our networks. We consider two mechanistically distinct ways of modelling chronic stress and how it reshapes excitatory-inhibitory dynamics, giving rise to eight stress operators. We perturb the network with a stress operator S either by rescaling recurrent synaptic weights, or by rescaling the gain on neural activity. In both cases a single magnitude δ sets the size of the perturbation. On average the operator multiplies its target element by 1 + δ, namely E[S[X]] = (1 + δ) X, so δ reads directly as a fractional change ( δ = 0 .1 is a 10% increase, δ = −0.1 a 10% decrease), while a fixed relative dispersion 

σstress adds heterogeneity across synapses or neurons. On the synaptic side we target two pathways: inhibition onto excitatory cells ( S↑[WI→E ], rescaling WEI ) and recurrent excitation ( S↓[WE→E ], rescaling WEE ), benchmarked against non-selective controls that rescale all recurrent synapses up or down ( S↑[W ], S↓[W ]). On the activity side we apply a gain on each population’s output: inhibitory ( S↑[rI ]) or excitatory ( S↓[rE ]) gain, against non-selective controls applied to the whole network ( S↑[r], S↓[r]). The selective operators isolate the contribution of a specific pathway or cell class, whereas the non-selective controls quantify what follows simply from a global change in drive. We evaluate the network under each candidate stress operator S against three minimal, experimentally motivated signatures focused on the excitatory population of the circuit (Table 1): (i) inhibitory dominance observed at pyramidal neurons, quantified by the E/I ratio of recurrent drives hE→E /|hI→E |; (ii) excitatory hypofunction, quantified by rE

and hE→E ; and (iii) impaired task performance. A successful stress mechanism modelled as a perturbation to the network should reproduce all three signatures when applied, while the unstressed network remains within baseline ranges. 

Resilience training. Throughout the Results we contrast two groups of networks that differ only in their training, for details see Section 4.5: networks trained on the task alone, which we call naive , and networks trained with a stress instance of the same operator applied on every trial, which we call resilient . The nomenclature is motivated by the observation that the application of stress operator S reduces decision confidence in the naive, but not the resilient network trained under S. Each resilient network is specific to the stress operator S used for training and is not expected to generalise across operators. We evaluated 50 independently trained networks for each stress operator. The observation of reduced task decision confidence can be explained by the internal dynamics of the networks (Figure 1(e-f); inset values give the temporal mean of the trial-averaged change under the stress operator in percent of the baseline). In the naive network the stress operator drives a large, near-uniform suppression of excitatory and inhibitory populations: the excitatory firing rate ¯rE falls by 79 .0% , the recurrent excitatory drive ¯hE→E falls by 77 .9% , the absolute inhibitory drive |¯hI→E |

and 35 .5% , respectively, while the inhibitory drive is increased ( +7 .9% ), giving a 41 .0% decrease in the E/ |I| ratio, Figure 1(f). Resilience training thus leads to selective suppression of excitatory inputs under stress operators instead of the global suppression of neuronal inputs observed in naive networks.

S↑[WI→E ] with δ = 0 .25 , performance of the resilient networks hold near-baseline accuracy across task difficulties, whereas naive networks lose accuracy, Fig. 2(a) right. The comparison task becomes increasingly difficult with increasing time delay between S1 and S2 presentation. Testing delays beyond the range used under resilience training reveals a generalisation trade-off: While resilient networks perform similar to or better than naive networks under delays used during training, performance decreases for longer delays: Without stress at test time, resilient networks degrade faster than naive networks at long, out-of-distribution delays (Fig. 2(b), left). Under stress the naive networks converge to the same poor out-of-distribution accuracy, so the two classes converge at long delays while resilient networks keep their full advantage only inside the trained range (Fig. 2(b), right). The trade-off is therefore one of flexibility rather than directly of task error: the specialization of the resilient network to the more complex conditions under training leads to reduced extrapolation. This mirrors a higher-level signature of chronic stress in animals, which tend toward habit-like, inflexible behaviour and adapt poorly to novel task demands. 2.1 Systematic comparison of stress mechanisms 

We discuss the upscaling of inhibitory-to-excitatory weights, stress operator S↑[WI→E ], because it stands out against alternative stress operators as the mechanism that best reproduces the target signatures of Table 1. We now compare the stress operator S↑[WI→E ] to alternative stress operators. For each stress operator S, we evaluate task performance and network drive for naive networks and resilient networks trained on S, for details see Section 4.6. Figure 3 reports each operator’s percentage change comparing task performance (panel a) and internal dynamics (panel b) between tasks with and without stress operator, in naive and resilient networks, with the two values per cell giving the change at the training stress magnitude of δT = 0 .5 and a larger stress beyond the training magnitude with δmax = 1 .0.The experimental literature supports chronic stress inducing inhibitory dominance through elevated inhibitory drive onto pyramidal cells [ 15 , 16 , 14 ], so we ask which operators lower the E/I ratio while keeping or raising the inhibitory drive to excitatory cells, |hI→E |, see Table 1. S↑[WI→E ] discussed so far is the natural candidate: it scales only the 

I → E weights, strengthening inhibition onto excitatory cells directly, so any drop in excitation follows from their suppression rather than from touching excitatory synapses. Whether the resulting inhibitory drive then holds steady or rises depends on how much inhibitory activity the circuit sustains, which is where naive and resilient networks differ. We discuss in the following our observations for all stress operators in comparison to the experimental observations from Table 1. On naive networks, all four targeted operators lower the E/I ratio by pulling both drives down, with hE→E falling faster, and they differ mainly in strength (Fig. 3(b), naive). S↑[WI→E ], S↓[WE→E ] and S↓[rE ] each produce a strong phenotype ( hE→E and ¯rE down 60% to 88% , E/ |I| down 48% to 70% , accuracy down 32% to 38% ; Fig. 3(a)), but they differ in how far they drag inhibition down: S↑[WI→E ] holds |hI→E | to a 39% drop at δT , whereas S↓[WE→E ]

and S↓[rE ] pull it down 56% to 58% , a more uniform shutdown. S↑[rI ] is the mildest operator of all, changing every readout the least ( ¯rE −30% , E/ |I| − 22% , accuracy −14% ) and leaving |hI→E | with a decrease of −9% . Neither control condition, in which weights or neuronal activities are scaled simultaneously for both excitatory and inhibitory populations, reproduces the expected phenotype from Table 1: the up-controls push every drive past +100% with E/ |I|

slightly positive, while the down-controls lower both drives at δT (E/ |I| − 18% ) but turn non-monotonic by δmax ,where ¯rE and E/ |I| flip positive ( +72 to +84% and +46 to +48% ). We can thus exclude non-selective scaling of neuronal drive or activity as biologically realistic stress operator. 2.3.1 Resilient networks preserve baseline population geometry under stress

The dimensionality of the 90% -variance baseline subspace was nonetheless similar in the two groups ( ¯kNaive = 4 .12 ± 1.02 ,

Naive stressed trajectories shifted far from their unstressed baselines, whereas resilient trajectories showed only a controlled shift that preserved trajectory shape. To quantify displacement systematically, we projected trajectories into the top-k baseline subspace and measured the mean Euclidean displacement of stressed trajectories from their baseline counterparts (Fig. 5b). Displacement was roughly 60 to 70% smaller in resilient networks at both perturbation strengths ( 1.34 versus 0.45 at δ = 0 .25 ; 1.80 versus 0.73 at δ = 0 .5; both p < 10 −16 ). displacement within each class (both p < 10 −10 ), yet the effect of training dominated: resilient networks at the stronger perturbation remained less displaced than naive networks at the weaker one ( 0.73 versus 1.34 ; p < 10 −15 ). (Methods, Section 4.7), defined as the fraction of stressed-activity variance captured by the baseline PCA subspace. 2.3.2 Resilient networks maintain baseline energetic organisation under stress 

We then asked whether resilient networks also preserved their energetic regime under stress. For each condition we computed the temporally resolved metabolic energy E(t) as a weighted sum of neuronal activity and synaptic transmission, E(t) = α1R(t) + α2[SE (t) + SI (t)] [41 ], where R is the population firing-rate cost and SE , SI are the synaptic input costs onto excitatory and inhibitory populations, respectively. Energy components were computed from the effective stress-perturbed weights and averaged over trials and time (Methods, Section 4.8); smaller stress-induced changes indicate that a network maintains its baseline energetic regime. At baseline, resilient networks operated at lower total energy than naive networks ( E = 0 .729 versus 0.845 ; p = 0 .013 ;Fig. 5d). Under stress the two diverged sharply: Naive energy collapsed ( E = 0 .501 at δ = 0 .25 , 0.390 at δ = 0 .5;both versus baseline p < 10 −11 ), whereas Resilient energy stayed close to baseline ( E = 0 .680 and 0.652 ). To quantify energy-state preservation independent of baseline differences, we computed the per-seed relative deviation 

|Eδ − EBL |/E BL , which was several-fold smaller in resilient networks ( 0.068 versus 0.382 at δ = 0 .25 ; 0.108 versus 

0.511 at δ = 0 .5; both p < 10 −17 ) and significantly less variable across seeds (Brown–Forsythe, both p < 10 −8). Decomposing the synaptic energy showed that the naive collapse reflected a broad loss of recurrent synaptic cost, whereas resilient networks preserved it. Because SE measures synaptic transmission cost received by excitatory neurons, the stress-induced rise in I → E drive is contained in its inhibitory-source component SI→E . Consistent with the drive analysis, SI→E fell in naive networks ( 28 .4% at δ = 0 .25 , 36 .7% at δ = 0 .5) but was slightly elevated in resilient networks ( +2 .5% and +5 .7% ; naive versus resilient p < 10 −17 ). The increased I → E drive in resilient networks therefore carries a corresponding synaptic transmission cost onto the excitatory population. Together, these analyses show that resilient networks preserve both the geometric and energetic structure of baseline recurrent activity under stress, whereas naïve networks undergo a dynamical and energetic collapse.

3 Discussion 

Using Dale-constrained recurrent networks trained on a delayed parametric working-memory task, we modelled chronic stress as an external, circuit-level perturbation. We first asked which perturbation best reproduces the experimentally reported signatures of stress-induced prefrontal dysfunction. Across eight candidate stress operators, selectively increasing inhibitory-to-excitatory synapses, S↑[WI→E ], stood out as the only individual mechanism that jointly produced inhibitory dominance, excitatory hypofunction, and impaired performance, fitting experimental reports. Treating stress as an external operator reflects that it is fundamentally a bodily response which cooperates with neural circuits rather than a property of those circuits alone [ 1 , 8]. This is a simplification that separates the circuit-level consequences of a stress-induced perturbation from the processes that generate it. Our finding that one stress mechanism reproduced both inhibitory dominance and excitatory hypofunction shows that different observations on inhibitory dominance in prefrontal pyramidal cells – elevated PV interneuron activity [ 14 ], more GABAergic contacts onto pyramidal cells [ 15 ], and subregion-specific increases in inhibition and decreases in excitation [ 16 ] – might well form a causal cascade with excitatory hypofunction emerging as a downstream consequence rather than an imposed alteration, instead of acting in parallel. We then studied how networks adapt to the stress operator via resilience training. Comparing networks trained with and without applying the stress operator, naive and resilient, revealed that resilience training preserves task performance under stress but introduces a delay-generalisation trade-off. Furthermore, resilient networks show decreased network density and reciprocity, and absorb the stress operator while preserving their geometric dynamics and energetic structure observed without stress. Reduced density indicates that resilience training is associated with a more selective pattern of effective recurrent connectivity. Lower weighted reciprocity further suggests a shift away from symmetric bidirectional interactions toward a more directional recurrent information flow. These observations are consistent with the broader picture emerging from the present study, in which resilience training preserve performance under stress operators, but exhibit reduced delay generalisation outside the training distribution. Resilient networks concentrate their activity and energy in a compact region near baseline. This compact organisation is what protects them, but it is also the dynamical side of their specialisation: a network tuned tightly to its trained regime handles the stress operator it was trained on, yet copes poorly with the longer, untrained delays, which is where the generalisation cost appears. Notably, energetic stability was not part of the training objective; resilient networks were optimised only to perform under the perturbation, yet they preserved their baseline metabolic regime as a byproduct, suggesting that energetic organisation and dynamical robustness are linked in these solutions. As a behavioural hypothesis, the trade-off offers a new reading of stress-induced rigidity: in chronically stressed animals [ 6 , 7 ], the habit-like solution that preserves performance on familiar tasks may be the very thing that weakens generalisation to novel ones, recasting inflexibility as the cost of resilience rather than a general deficit, even without acute stress. Together, the findings suggest that resilience training is accompanied by a reorganization of recurrent connectivity toward architectures specialized for maintaining function under chronic stress-like conditions, potentially at the expense of computational flexibility and generalization capacity. This study illustrates the value of training biologically constrained recurrent networks to study stress [ 22 , 34 ]. By imposing a minimal mechanistic constraint, here an E/I network obeying Dale’s principle, we can ask how stress affects that specific component while retaining complete observation of the system’s activity, drives, and energetics, a level of access unavailable in vivo . Because the network performs a cognitive function, working memory, the effect of stress operators can be related directly to behaviourally relevant computation. More broadly, the approach opens experimental directions for understanding the circuit and cellular mechanisms of stress: it generates qualitative, testable signatures, such as the asymmetric drive pattern above, and may reveal mechanisms that can in turn be built into biologically inspired artificial systems to account for stress in a principled way. 

Limitations. A strength of the RNN approach, and equally its main caveat, is that we control every part of the system: its architecture and temporal activity (the biology), the data it is trained on (the environment), and the rule by which it learns (the learning mechanism) [ 42 , 43 ]. This control is what makes the learned mechanisms interpretable, but it also means that apparently minor design choices can change both performance and the solution the network adopts [ 44 ]. Our results should therefore be read as properties of a deliberately simplified model of cognition, one that, unlike a behaving animal, carries no experiential history beyond its training [ 45 ]. The model also enforces a strict Dale’s principle, so that each neuron either excites or inhibits; while a widely used first-order approximation, real neurons engage in co-transmission and neuromodulation, releasing multiple transmitters and recruiting parallel fast and slow signalling pathways [ 46 ], so biological circuits carry greater chemical and functional complexity than captured here. Because our study explicitly models biological alterations observed under chronic stress, our networks are not applicable to study acute stress. 11 Chronic stress as an E/I perturbation in RNNs A P REPRINT 

Future directions. Several extensions follow directly. We treated stress as an external operator; modelling how such a perturbation arises from internal plasticity mechanisms, as the network’s own response to stressful stimuli, would close the loop between the stressor, the bodily response, and the circuit change. We also addressed resilience only in the sense of maintained function under stress; its complementary sense, rapid recovery of function after a stress-induced decline, maps onto online adaptation and recovery dynamics that our static-perturbation setting does not capture. Each operator was applied independently, whereas stress in the organism is a coordinated set of processes acting across molecular, endocrine, and circuit scales [ 47 ]; combinations of operators may correspond to distinct physiological processes, and characterising how they interact is a promising direction. Finally, we characterised the delay-generalisation trade-off in a single categorical-decision task; whether it generalises across task structures, in particular to memories supported by continuous attractors, remains open. Addressing these would extend the present framework further toward biologically inspired models of stress and stress inoculation.

4.1 Network model 

We follow the formulation of Song et al. [22] , using a rate-based recurrent neural network of excitatory and inhibitory populations. 4.2 Delayed parametric comparison task implementation 

The delayed parametric comparison task adapts the parametric working-memory paradigm in which prefrontal delay activity encodes remembered stimulus values [ 50 , 51 ]. Each network received three input channels and produced two output channels. The input channels encoded fixation, the first scalar stimulus S1, and the second scalar stimulus S2,respectively. The two output channels encoded the choices S1 > S 2 and S2 > S 1. Trials used a time-step of ∆t = 20 

ms. 12 Chronic stress as an E/I perturbation in RNNs A P REPRINT 

Each trial lasted 2000 ms, corresponding to T = 100 simulation steps. Trials began with a 400 ms fixation epoch. The first stimulus was then presented for 250 ms and the second stimulus for 250 ms. The delay between S1 and S2 was sampled uniformly from 400–900 ms. The decision epoch began immediately after the second stimulus and continued until the end of the trial. Stimulus amplitudes were sampled from the interval S1, S 2 ∈ [0 .2, 1.2] . Trial difficulty was controlled by the signed difference ∆ = S2 − S1. For training, difference magnitudes were sampled from |∆| ∈{0, 0.032 , 0.064 , 0.128 , 0.256 , 0.512 }. For non-zero differences, the sign was chosen randomly, so trials included both S1 > S 2 and S2 > S 1 comparisons. For a given sampled ∆, S1 was drawn uniformly from the range that allowed 

S2 = S1 + ∆ to remain within the stimulus bounds. The effective difference was then recorded as the realised difference 

S2 − S1. The fixation channel was active from trial onset until the decision epoch and a tonic baseline input of 0.2

was added to all input channels throughout the trial. Input noise was added independently across time points and input channels, sampled log-uniformly from σin ∈ [0 .01 , 0.05] and rectified to be non-negative. Targets are zero during the trial time span except only during the decision period. If S1 > S 2, the target was [1 , 0] ; if 

S2 > S 1, the target was [0 , 1] . For exact ties ( S1 = S2), the target was [0 .5, 0.5] . The training mask assigned full loss weight during fixation and decision periods. The intervening stimulus and delay periods were assigned a reduced mask weight of wstim = 0 .01 , so that training emphasised fixation maintenance and final choice accuracy while only weakly constraining network output during stimulus presentation and memory maintenance.

4.5 Stress training 

We compare two training regimes. Under standard training the recurrent weights are left unperturbed. Under stress training, each trial draws its own stress instance, with magnitude δ ∼ U [0 , δ T ], held fixed throughout that trial’s forward pass. The stress operator S is then applied to its target according to the mechanism under study, perturbing either the weights in θ or the population activity r(t), and the task loss is computed on the output of the resulting perturbed forward pass. Because the perturbation is an element-wise product by sampled factors that are held fixed, it is differentiable in the underlying quantities, and gradients flow back to θ whether the operator acts on weights or on activity. Drawing a new stress instance each trial, the network learns to solve the task robustly across perturbations of magnitude up to δT ,rather than under a single unperturbed configuration. 

4.6 Systematic stress mechanism comparison 

We evaluated all eight operators on both naive and resilient networks at three magnitudes δ ∈ { 0, 0.5, 1.0} (negated for downscaling protocols): the unstressed baseline, the maximum training magnitude δT , and the maximum tested magnitude δmax . 4.9 Delay-generalisation tradeoff analysis 

We evaluated whether training for stress resilience altered temporal generalisation on the parametric working-memory task. During training, delays were sampled from 400 –900 ms . We therefore defined delays in this interval as within-distribution delays, and delays longer than 900 ms as long out-of-distribution delays, denoted OOD-high. For each trained network, accuracy was averaged separately over within-distribution and OOD-high delays. Accuracy was computed from 2000 trials per delay condition, excluding zero-coherence trials. A trial was counted as correct when the sign of the mean decision variable during the decision period matched the sign of the stimulus difference. We analysed two generalisation tradeoff experiments. In the training stress magnitude experiment, Resilient networks were trained with maximum stress magnitude δmax ∈ { 0.25 , 0.5, 0.75 , 1.0, 1.5, 2.0}. For each value of δmax , 50 

independently trained networks were evaluated for each network type, Naïve and Resilient. In the network size experiment, we tested N ∈ { 50 , 100 , 200 , 400 , 500 }, with the excitatory population fixed at Nexc = 0 .8N , rounded to the nearest integer. Again, for each network size, 50 independently trained networks were evaluated for each network type.
